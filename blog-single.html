Sharing: Articles and Some Ideas about Square Word Calligraphy

by Teddy / review

Willing to share two articles that I read today.

One is a book review from Michael D. Mathiowetz and Andrew D. Turner about the close relationship of flowers to the religion and culture of Mesoamerica and the American Southwest. Michael D. Mathiowetz, Andrew D. Turner. 2022. Flower Worlds: Religion, Aesthetics, and Ideology in Mesoamerica and the American Southwest

Another is from Nathan H. Madson, who provides a human rights perspective on the human rights issues and issues of regional belonging faced by underrepresented groups in Hong Kong, particularly LGBTQ+. Madson, Nathan H. 2022. I am an ordinary citizen: Human rights discourse and the limits of human rights law.

"The situation in Hong Kong may be the result of a unique history of colonialism, transfer of sovereignty, and ideologies of Hong Kong exceptionalism vis-à-vis the mainland Chinese, but that does not mean that the ways in which human rights discourse is used to shore up an ordinary Hongkonger identity cannot be instructive beyond the territory."

When I created my own website, I was conflicted. I considered the issue of attribution of square word calligraphy. This topic was briefly discussed in my short essay Comments On Dadaism By Square Word Calligraphy. It's a mission with no rules, therefore we're stuck in a "comply and break" cycle. It is difficult for authorities to characterize as a criminal conduct. Art is a spiritual activity that necessitates certain foundations; it is both fascinating and perilous to utilize an art form that arises out of nowhere, is new, or to join an entirely new cultural genre. It has the potential to be fetishized. In this situation, a free mind and a confined background swing back and forth, which reminds me of Warburg's idea of swinging, or possibly the notion stated by Edgar Wind. Perhaps, even in our unique national setting, both square word calligraphy and modern art in all its forms might exhibit oscillation. I hope that more work which does not burst can emerge in the Chinese art scene; after all, we are all bored of the same themes and shapes. Our culture should shift away from depending on exports and toward inside-out, confident inclusion.
